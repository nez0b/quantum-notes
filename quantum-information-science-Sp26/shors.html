The Factoring Problem and RSA

Shor's algorithm exists because one specific computational problem simultaneously underwrites trillions of dollars of internet commerce and admits an exponential quantum speedup. That problem is integer factoring.

The setup. Alice picks two large primes \(p, q\), roughly \(1024\)-bit each, and publishes \(N = pq\) along with a small encryption exponent \(e\) coprime to \(\varphi(N) = (p-1)(q-1)\). To send Alice a message \(m \in \mathbb{Z}_N\), Bob transmits \(c = m^e \bmod N\). Alice recovers \(m\) by computing \(c^d \bmod N\) where \(d = e^{-1} \bmod \varphi(N)\). The catch: computing \(d\) requires \(\varphi(N)\), and \(\varphi(N)\) is essentially as hard as factoring \(N\).

Why factoring is "hard". The best known classical algorithm — the general number field sieve — runs in \[ \exp\!\bigl((1.92 + o(1))\,(\ln N)^{1/3} (\ln\ln N)^{2/3}\bigr), \] which is sub-exponential but super-polynomial. RSA-250 (829 bits) was factored in 2020 with about 2700 CPU-core-years; RSA-2048 sits well beyond the reach of all classical hardware that will ever be built.

Shor's contribution is to factor \(N\) in \(O((\log N)^3)\) quantum time and \(O((\log N)^2)\) qubits — polynomial in the length of the input. Every public-key system whose security reduces to factoring or discrete log dies the day a fault-tolerant quantum computer comes online.

The trapdoor and why Shor breaks it

RSA is a trapdoor: encrypting is fast, decrypting is fast for someone who knows \(p, q\), and inverting (without the trapdoor) is conjectured hard. Shor doesn't break the cipher directly; he factors \(N\), which gives \(\varphi(N)\), which gives \(d\). Below, the entire reduction is laid bare.

Detailed math: the RSA decryption identity

For \(\gcd(m, N) = 1\), Euler's theorem gives \(m^{\varphi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod N\). Pick \(d\) with \(ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\varphi(N)}\), so \(ed = 1 + k\varphi(N)\) for some integer \(k\). Then \[ \begin{aligned} c^d &\equiv (m^e)^d = m^{ed} = m^{1+k\varphi(N)} \\ &\equiv m \cdot (m^{\varphi(N)})^k \equiv m \pmod N. \end{aligned} \] The Chinese remainder theorem extends the identity to \(\gcd(m, N) > 1\) as well.

Key Points

  • RSA security \(\Leftrightarrow\) factoring \(N = pq\).
  • Classical NFS: sub-exponential, not polynomial.
  • Shor: polynomial in \(\log N\) on a quantum computer.
  • Knowing \(\varphi(N)\) is equivalent to knowing \(p, q\).

Number-Theory Toolkit

Three classical ingredients power both RSA and Shor: Euclid's GCD algorithm, Euler's theorem (with the totient \(\varphi\)), and modular exponentiation by repeated squaring. The calculator below walks through the binary expansion of the exponent.

For \(N = pq\), \(\varphi(N) = (p-1)(q-1)\). The multiplicative group is \(\mathbb{Z}_N^\times = \{x : 1 \le x < N,\, \gcd(x, N) = 1\}\), and \(|\mathbb{Z}_N^\times| = \varphi(N)\). Fermat–Euler says \(x^{\varphi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod N\) for any \(x \in \mathbb{Z}_N^\times\). The order of \(x\) is the smallest positive \(s\) with \(x^s \equiv 1 \pmod N\); it always divides \(\varphi(N)\).

Computing \(x^r \bmod N\) by writing \(r = \sum b_i 2^i\) and iterating \(y \leftarrow y^2,\, y \leftarrow y \cdot x\) takes \(O(\log r)\) modular multiplications — i.e. polynomial in \(\log N\). Shor uses this same routine, made reversible, as the quantum oracle for \(f(r) = x^r \bmod N\).

Reducing Factoring to Period-Finding

The classical half of Shor's algorithm is a clever observation: if you can find the multiplicative order of a random element \(x \in \mathbb{Z}_N^\times\), you can factor \(N\) with high probability.

The reduction. Pick \(x\) uniformly from \(\{2, \ldots, N-1\}\). If \(\gcd(x, N) > 1\), we already have a non-trivial factor (a giveaway that almost never happens for \(N = pq\)). Otherwise, find the order \(s\) of \(x\), the smallest \(s \ge 1\) with \(x^s \equiv 1 \pmod N\). If \(s\) is even and \(x^{s/2} \not\equiv -1 \pmod N\), then \[ (x^{s/2} - 1)(x^{s/2} + 1) = x^s - 1 \equiv 0 \pmod N, \] so \(N\) divides the product but divides neither factor, meaning both \(\gcd(N, x^{s/2} - 1)\) and \(\gcd(N, x^{s/2} + 1)\) are non-trivial factors of \(N\).

The "lucky-twice" 3/8 bound

Two things must go right: \(s\) must be even, and the resulting \(x^{s/2}\) must not equal \(-1 \pmod N\). For \(N\) a product of two odd primes, a careful Chinese-remainder argument shows \[ \Pr_{x \in \mathbb{Z}_N^\times}[s \text{ even and } x^{s/2} \not\equiv -1] \ge \tfrac{3}{8}. \] So a constant number of random restarts suffices, and the bulk of the work is the order-finding subroutine — which is exactly what the quantum part of Shor handles in polynomial time.

Classical order-finding is roughly as hard as factoring. The entire quantum advantage of Shor's algorithm lives in this single subroutine.
Detailed math: the \(3/8\) lower bound via CRT

Write \(\mathbb{Z}_N^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}_p^\times \times \mathbb{Z}_q^\times\) by CRT. Each \(\mathbb{Z}_p^\times\) is cyclic of even order \(p-1\). Let \(s_p, s_q\) be the orders of the projections of \(x\). Then \(s = \mathrm{lcm}(s_p, s_q)\). Failure (either \(s\) odd, or \(x^{s/2} \equiv -1\)) corresponds to \(s_p\) and \(s_q\) having the same 2-adic valuation. A counting argument over the cyclic factors gives \(\Pr[\text{failure}] \le 1/2 + 1/4 = \cdots\); the clean bound that survives is \(\Pr[\text{success}] \ge 3/8\). See Aaronson L19 for the full case analysis.

Key Points

  • Order-finding \(\Rightarrow\) factoring with prob \(\ge 3/8\).
  • Failure modes: \(s\) odd, or \(x^{s/2} \equiv -1\).
  • Both factors fall out of two GCDs.
  • Constant restarts suffice classically.

QFT Intuition: Combs In, Peaks Out

The quantum Fourier transform on \(\mathbb{Z}_Q\) is the unitary \[ F_Q\,\ket{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt Q}\sum_{k=0}^{Q-1} \omega^{jk}\,\ket{k}, \qquad \omega = e^{2\pi i / Q}. \] Apply it to a periodic "comb" with period \(s\) and the output concentrates on the multiples of \(Q/s\) (when \(s \mid Q\)).

Input: comb of period \(s\)

Output: \(F_Q\) applied

Phase clocks: where each input term points

Each dial shows the phase \(\omega^{jk}\) for output index \(j = j^{\star}\) (the largest output peak) and \(k\) ranging over the input support. When all hands align, you get a peak; when they cancel uniformly, you get destructive interference.

Detailed math: \(s \mid Q\) gives perfect peaks

If the input is the uniform comb \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{Q/s}} \sum_{m=0}^{Q/s - 1} \ket{r_0 + ms}\), then \[ \begin{aligned} F_Q\ket{\text{comb}} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt Q \sqrt{Q/s}} \sum_{j,m} \omega^{j(r_0 + ms)} \ket{j} \\ &= \frac{\omega^{j r_0}}{\sqrt{Q/s}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt Q} \sum_j \biggl(\sum_m \omega^{jms}\biggr)\ket{j}. \end{aligned} \] The inner geometric sum equals \(Q/s\) when \(j \equiv 0 \pmod{Q/s}\) and \(0\) otherwise. Squaring: each peak has probability \(s/Q \cdot (Q/s) = 1/s\) — there are \(s\) peaks, each equiprobable. Measuring \(j\) reveals a uniformly random multiple of \(Q/s\).

Inside the Black Box: Implementing the QFT

The QFT is not magic — it is a recursive circuit of \(O(\log^2 Q)\) gates. The key identity is \[ F_{2N} = \frac{1}{\sqrt 2} \begin{pmatrix} F_N & B_N \\ F_N & -B_N \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B_N = \mathrm{diag}(1, \omega_{2N}, \omega_{2N}^2, \ldots). \] This is the same divide-and-conquer that gives the classical FFT, but realized as a quantum circuit acting on \(n = \log_2 Q\) qubits.

On qubits, the recipe is: Hadamard on the most significant qubit, controlled phase rotations \(R_k = \mathrm{diag}(1, e^{2\pi i / 2^k})\) from the lower qubits onto it, then recurse on the remaining qubits. A final cascade of SWAPs reverses the bit order. For three qubits (\(Q = 8\)) the circuit reads:

Gate count

Counting: the top qubit costs \(1\) Hadamard plus \(n-1\) controlled phases; recursion on \(n-1\) qubits gives the rest. Hence \[ C(n) = C(n-1) + n = O(n^2) = O(\log^2 Q), \] plus \(\lfloor n/2 \rfloor\) SWAPs at the end. Coppersmith's approximate QFT drops phase gates with rotation angle below \(2\pi / 2^{\log n}\); the resulting circuit has \(O(n \log n)\) gates and changes the output distribution by only an inverse-polynomial amount — which is enough for Shor.

Detailed math: deriving the controlled-phase pattern

Write \(\ket{j} = \ket{j_{n-1} j_{n-2} \cdots j_0}\). A direct expansion of \(F_Q\ket{j}\) yields the product form \[ F_Q\ket{j} = \frac{1}{\sqrt Q} \prod_{\ell=1}^{n} \bigl(\ket{0} + e^{2\pi i \cdot 0.j_{\ell-1} j_{\ell-2} \cdots j_0}\ket{1}\bigr), \] where the binary fraction \(0.j_{\ell-1}\cdots j_0 = j_{\ell-1}/2 + \cdots + j_0/2^\ell\). Each tensor factor is produced by one Hadamard and a cascade of controlled-\(R_k\) gates from below — exactly the circuit above (modulo the bit-reversing SWAPs).

Key Points

  • Recursive: \(F_{2N}\) from \(F_N\) plus phase block.
  • \(O(\log^2 Q)\) gates exactly.
  • Coppersmith approximation: \(O(n \log n)\) gates suffice.
  • Final SWAPs reverse bit order.

After QFT and measurement we hold a pair \((j, Q)\) with \(j/Q \approx k/s\) for some unknown \(k\) and the period \(s\) we want. Why \(Q = \Theta(N^2)\)?

The continued-fractions step needs to recover \(k/s\) from a measurement \(j\) with \(j/Q\) within \(1/(2Q)\) of the true \(k/s\). Uniqueness of the rational reconstruction requires \(Q\) at least quadratic in \(N\).

The uniqueness theorem. For any \(x \in [0, 1]\) and \(\delta > 0\), at most one rational \(a/b\) with \(b \le 1/\sqrt{2\delta}\) satisfies \(|x - a/b| \le \delta\). Take \(\delta = 1/(2Q)\): we need \(s \le 1/\sqrt{1/Q} = \sqrt Q\), so we want \(\sqrt Q \ge N\), i.e. \(Q \ge N^2\). Standard practice picks \(Q\) to be the smallest power of two with \(N^2 \le Q < 2N^2\).

Why not just use \(Q = N\)? Multiple distinct rationals \(a_1/b_1, a_2/b_2 < N\) can sit within the same \(1/(2Q)\) window — continued fractions cannot tell them apart. The quadratic blow-up is the price of unique reconstruction.

Mini-interactive: the uniqueness window

Slide \(Q\) from \(N\) up to \(N^2\). The orange band is the \(1/(2Q)\) window around \(k/Q\); blue dots are all rationals \(a/b\) with \(b \le N\). When \(Q\) is too small, several dots sit in the band; once \(Q \ge N^2\), only one survives.

Detailed math: triangle-inequality proof of uniqueness

Suppose \(a_1/b_1\) and \(a_2/b_2\) are distinct rationals with \(b_1, b_2 \le B\) and both within \(\delta\) of \(x\). Then by the triangle inequality \[ \left|\frac{a_1}{b_1} - \frac{a_2}{b_2}\right| \le 2\delta. \] But the left-hand side is at least \(1/(b_1 b_2) \ge 1/B^2\). So \(1/B^2 \le 2\delta\), i.e. \(B \ge 1/\sqrt{2\delta}\). Contrapositive: if \(b_1, b_2 < 1/\sqrt{2\delta}\), the two rationals must be equal.

Key Points

  • Window \(1/(2Q)\) determines reconstruction radius.
  • Uniqueness: at most one \(a/b\) with \(b \le \sqrt{1/(2\delta)}\).
  • So \(Q \ge N^2\) suffices.
  • Standard: \(N^2 \le Q < 2N^2\), \(Q\) a power of two.

Failure Modes and Restarts

Three things can go wrong in a single run of Shor. Each is fixable by re-randomizing the base \(x\) or re-running the quantum subroutine; the constant \(3/8\) success bound means \(O(1)\) restarts suffice in expectation.

(a) Order is odd

If \(s\) is odd, then \(x^{s/2}\) is not even an integer modulo \(N\), and the difference-of-squares trick fails. For a random \(x\), this happens with probability \(\le 1/2\). For \(N = 21\), \(x = 2\) has order \(6\) (even, OK), but \(x = 4\) has order \(3\) (odd, retry).

(b) \(x^{s/2} \equiv -1 \pmod N\)

Then \(\gcd(N, x^{s/2} - 1) \cdot \gcd(N, x^{s/2} + 1)\) puts both prime factors into the same gcd, leaving the trivial \(N\) and \(1\). Restart with a fresh \(x\). Example: for \(N = 15\), \(x = 4\) has \(s = 2\) and \(4^1 = 4 \not\equiv -1\): success. But \(x = 14\) has \(s = 2\) and \(14^1 \equiv -1 \pmod{15}\): failure.

(c) Measured \(j\) does not give \(s\)

The continued-fractions step might return \(k/s\) reduced to lowest terms — if \(\gcd(k, s) > 1\), the recovered denominator is too small. Mitigation: take a second sample \(k'/s\), and \(\mathrm{lcm}(\text{denominators})\) usually equals \(s\). In the \(N = 15\) walkthrough, sample \(j = 128 \to 1/2\) gives denom \(2\); sample \(j = 64 \to 1/4\) gives denom \(4\); the lcm is \(4 = s\).

A subtler edge case (HW10 Q1): a function \(f\) that is \(s\)-periodic in the algebraic sense (\(s \mid p - q \Leftrightarrow f(p) = f(q)\)) need not be a Shor-style modular exponential. The algorithm still recovers \(s\) because the QFT only cares about the shift-invariance, not about the specific values of \(f\).

All three failure modes are detectable from classical post-processing alone (parity check, gcd test, denominator bound). The total per-attempt success probability is at least \(3/8\); after a logarithmic number of attempts, failure probability is exponentially small.

Key Points

  • (a) odd \(s\): retry \(x\).
  • (b) \(x^{s/2} = -1\): retry \(x\).
  • (c) \(\gcd(k, s) > 1\): combine samples via lcm.
  • Per-attempt success \(\ge 3/8\).

Variant: Discrete Log and Diffie–Hellman

The same period-finding machinery, applied to a function on a two-dimensional lattice instead of \(\mathbb{Z}\), breaks the discrete-log problem and therefore Diffie–Hellman key exchange and elliptic-curve cryptography.

Setup. Fix a prime \(p\) and a generator \(\alpha\) of \(\mathbb{Z}_p^\times\) of order \(R\). Given \(g = \alpha^a \bmod p\), recover the secret exponent \(a\). Define \[ f(x_1, x_2) = \alpha^{x_1} g^{x_2} \bmod p = \alpha^{x_1 + a x_2} \bmod p. \] Then \(f(x_1, x_2) = f(x_1', x_2')\) iff \((x_1 - x_1') + a(x_2 - x_2') \equiv 0 \pmod R\). The hidden subgroup of \(\mathbb{Z}_R \times \mathbb{Z}_R\) is the line through the origin generated by \((-a, 1)\) — period vector \((\ell, -a\ell/\bmod R)\) for any integer \(\ell\).

Recovering \(a\)

Apply the QFT to both coordinates of register \(1\), then measure. By the same comb-to-peaks argument, you obtain a random pair \((z_1, z_2)\) lying on the dual lattice — i.e. \(z_1 \cdot \ell + z_2 \cdot (-a\ell) \equiv 0 \pmod R\), simplifying to \(z_1 \equiv a z_2 \pmod R\). If \(\gcd(z_2, R) = 1\), one extended-Euclidean step gives \(a = z_1 \cdot z_2^{-1} \bmod R\). Otherwise, retry — failure probability is \(O(1/\log R)\).

Diffie–Hellman, ElGamal, DSA, and elliptic-curve variants all reduce to discrete log on some abelian group. A scalable quantum computer breaks every one of them with the same two-register Shor variant.
Detailed math: HW10 Q3 walkthrough

After the QFT one measures \((z_1, z_2)\) uniformly on \(\{(z_1, z_2) : z_1 + a z_2 \equiv 0 \pmod R\}\). Pretend \(z_2\) is invertible mod \(R\). Then \(a \equiv -z_1 z_2^{-1} \pmod R\), and one application of the extended Euclidean algorithm yields \(a\) in polynomial time. The set of \(z_2\) coprime to \(R\) has density \(\varphi(R)/R = \Theta(1/\log\log R)\), so the success probability per run is at least \(\Omega(1/\log\log R)\) — and by classical amplification, a polylog number of runs suffice.

Key Points

  • Discrete log = period-finding on \(\mathbb{Z}_R^2\).
  • Hidden subgroup is the line \(\{(\ell, -a\ell)\}\).
  • Recover \(a\) by extended Euclidean.
  • Breaks DH, ElGamal, DSA, ECDSA.

The Hidden Subgroup Problem

Optional: HSP framework (collapsible)

Factoring, discrete log, and Simon's algorithm are all instances of the hidden subgroup problem (HSP). Given a group \(G\) and a function \(f : G \to S\) that is constant on the cosets of an unknown subgroup \(H \le G\) and distinct on different cosets, find \(H\). For finite abelian \(G\), Shor's QFT-over-\(G\) machinery solves HSP in polynomial time — a unified abstraction that subsumes everything above.

For factoring, \(G = \mathbb{Z}\), \(H = s\mathbb{Z}\), and \(f(r) = x^r \bmod N\). For discrete log, \(G = \mathbb{Z}_R \times \mathbb{Z}_R\) and \(H\) is the line \(\{(\ell, -a\ell) : \ell \in \mathbb{Z}_R\}\). For Simon's, \(G = \mathbb{Z}_2^n\) and \(H = \{0, s\}\). All three are abelian; Shor handles them all with the same QFT-then-measure recipe.

The non-abelian HSP is wide open. Solving HSP for the symmetric group \(S_n\) would yield an efficient quantum algorithm for graph isomorphism — and, separately, for the shortest vector problem on a lattice (which would break the NIST PQC standards). After thirty years of effort, no non-abelian polynomial-time HSP algorithm is known. Babai's 2016 quasi-polynomial classical algorithm for graph isomorphism partially deflated the urgency, but the non-abelian HSP remains the largest unsolved problem in quantum algorithm theory.

Key Points

  • HSP = unifying frame for Shor and Simon.
  • Abelian HSP: solved by QFT over \(G\).
  • Non-abelian HSP: open, would imply graph iso in BQP.

Where Shor Sits in Complexity Theory

Factoring is the canonical problem in the gap between classical and quantum polynomial time. Shor places it in BQP; classically it is conjectured to lie outside P. Together with the (mild) conjecture that Factoring \(\notin\) BPP, Shor's algorithm is the strongest evidence we have that BQP strictly contains BPP.

Where factoring sits. It is in NP (a witness is the prime factorization). It is in coNP (a primality certificate for each factor). It is therefore not NP-complete unless NP \(=\) coNP — so Shor is not "breaking NP", just beating one specific structured problem. And factoring is in BQP by Shor; conjecturally not in P (or even BPP). Hence Shor is the textbook witness that P \(\subsetneq\) BQP.

What Shor does not do

Shor does not solve NP-complete problems. SAT, 3-coloring, Hamiltonian cycle, integer programming — none of these have the algebraic period structure that the QFT exploits. The best-known quantum speedup for general NP-complete problems is Grover's quadratic improvement, not the exponential one Shor achieves on factoring.

"Quantum computers will solve everything faster" is a persistent misconception. The structured-problem speedups of Shor and HHL are dramatic but narrow; for unstructured search and most NP-complete problems, only Grover-style \(\sqrt N\) improvements are known.

Key Points

  • Factoring \(\in\) BQP \(\cap\) NP \(\cap\) coNP.
  • Conjecturally \(\notin\) P, hence BQP \(\ne\) BPP.
  • Shor \(\not\Rightarrow\) NP-complete in BQP.
  • Grover speedup is the generic NP fallback.

Shor at 30: The Algorithmic and Cryptographic Landscape

Shor's 1994 paper opened a thirty-year line of work that has shrunk the qubit budget, broadened the algorithm to new groups, and provoked the standardization of replacement cryptosystems. Ten signposts students should recognize:

1994 / 1997 · the original

Shor's algorithm (STOC 1994; SIAM J. Comput. 1997)

Polynomial-time quantum factoring and discrete log via period-finding plus the QFT over \(\mathbb{Z}_Q\). Subject of this entire tutorial.

1994 · Coppersmith

Approximate QFT

Drop phase rotations smaller than \(2\pi/2^{O(\log n)}\). Gate count drops from \(O(n^2)\) to \(O(n \log n)\) with inverse-polynomial error — fully sufficient for Shor.

1995 · Kitaev

Quantum phase estimation

Reframes Shor as estimating the eigenphase of the unitary \(U_x : \ket{y} \mapsto \ket{xy \bmod N}\). Phase estimation is now the mother subroutine for HHL, ground state energy, and amplitude estimation.

2003 · Beauregard

2n+3 qubit modular-exponentiation circuit

Showed Shor on an \(n\)-bit number needs only \(2n + 3\) logical qubits — a tight optimization that made resource-estimation papers possible. arXiv:quant-ph/0205095

2002 · Hallgren

Pell's equation in BQP

Extended Shor's framework to compute the regulator of a real quadratic field — i.e. solve Pell's equation \(x^2 - Dy^2 = 1\) — in quantum polynomial time. Best classical algorithm is sub-exponential.

2021 · Gidney–Ekerå

RSA-2048 resource estimate

Concrete physical resource cost for breaking RSA-2048: roughly \(20\) million noisy qubits running for \(8\) hours, assuming surface-code error correction. State of the art for "how hard would it actually be?". arXiv:1905.09749

2024 · Regev

Sub-exponential improvement

Replaces the single modular-exponentiation register with a \(d\)-dimensional lattice variant; reduces the number of modular multiplications from \(O(n^2)\) to \(O(n^{3/2})\) using \(O(n^{1/2})\)-bit lattice basis gymnastics. arXiv:2308.06572

2024 · Chevignard–Fouque–Schrottenloher

Regev follow-ups

Tightened analyses, memory–time trade-offs, and fault-tolerant resource estimates for the Regev variant. Shows the post-Regev landscape is still being mapped.

Aug 2024 · NIST

Post-quantum cryptography standards

FIPS 203 (ML-KEM, née Kyber), FIPS 204 (ML-DSA, née Dilithium), FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA, née SPHINCS+) — the first standardized replacements for RSA and ECDH, all based on lattice or hash-based hardness assumptions Shor cannot touch. FALCON (FN-DSA) standardization followed in 2025.

2020 / 2025 · records

Largest factored composites

Classical (NFS): RSA-250 (829 bits, 2020). Real quantum hardware: stuck near \(N = 21\) on bare-metal devices; "factoring \(N = 15\)" demos are routine. The four-order-of- magnitude gap is the fault-tolerance barrier.

Common Pitfalls

A short list of confusions that trip up students every year.

Doesn't QFT just compute the classical FFT?

It computes the same linear map, but on amplitudes rather than data. Classically, an \(N\)-point FFT takes \(O(N \log N)\) operations and produces all \(N\) output values. The QFT acts on \(n = \log N\) qubits in \(O(n^2)\) gates and produces a quantum state whose amplitudes are the FFT outputs — but you can only sample from it. The whole game of Shor is to design a problem where one sample is enough.

Why doesn't measurement destroy the period?

Measuring register 2 (the output of the modular exponentiation) collapses register 1 to a uniform superposition over the preimage \(\{r : x^r \equiv y \pmod N\}\) — which is exactly a comb of period \(s\) (offset \(r_0\)). The QFT then converts the period in the comb into a peak in frequency space, so measurement of register 1 reveals (a multiple of) \(Q/s\) with high probability.

Why \(Q = \Theta(N^2)\) and not \(Q = N\)?

See §9. Continued fractions can recover \(s\) from \(j/Q\) only if there is a unique rational approximation with denominator \(< N\) within the measurement window \(1/(2Q)\). The triangle-inequality bound forces \(Q \ge N^2\).

Couldn't classical computers just sample multiples of \(Q/s\)?

If you knew \(s\), yes. The point is that \(s\) is what you are trying to find. Classical Monte Carlo can sample \(f(r)\) but cannot interfere the samples — the multiples of \(Q/s\) only emerge as constructive interference after the QFT, an inherently quantum operation.

Does Shor's algorithm need millions of qubits today?

For RSA-2048, yes (Gidney–Ekerå 2021: \(\sim 20\) million noisy qubits, surface code, \(\sim 8\) hours). For toy instances like \(N = 15\) or \(N = 21\), bare-metal experiments have been done on a few qubits. The chasm between toy and threat-model is the cost of fault-tolerant error correction, not the cost of Shor's logical circuit.

Key Points

  • QFT \(\ne\) FFT: amplitudes vs data, sampling vs readout.
  • Measurement of register 2 creates the comb.
  • Resource estimates: millions of qubits, not thousands.
